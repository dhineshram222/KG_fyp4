Aloha is a Random Access protocol used in OSI models for wireless and shared mediums where multiple devices transmit data. In Pure Aloha, stations send frames at any time, which frequently causes collisions that destroy data. When a collision occurs, senders wait a random back-off time before retransmitting to reduce the chance of further conflict. Slotted Aloha improves this by dividing time into synchronized slots, allowing transmissions only at the start of each slot. This synchronization reduces collisions and increases the maximum efficiency from 18% in Pure Aloha to 37%. Pure Aloha's vulnerable time is twice the frame transmission time, and even a single bit of overlap between frames can result in total data destruction. Consequently, Slotted Aloha provides more reliable successful transmission. Both protocols generally rely on acknowledgments to confirm the receiver has successfully obtained the frame. Pure Aloha was introduced by the University of Hawaii, while Slotted Aloha was later introduced by Robert in 1972.
