A stack is a linear Abstract Data Type (ADT) where all insertions and deletions occur at a single end called the top. It operates on the Last-In-First-Out (LIFO) principle, meaning the most recently added item is the first to be removed. The primary operations are push, which adds an element, and pop, which removes the top element. Users can also use peek or top to view the topmost element without actually removing it from the stack. Structural constraints mean elements are accessed in reverse order of their arrival, similar to a stack of books. Status checks like isEmpty and isFull are used to prevent underflow (popping from an empty stack) or overflow (pushing to a full stack). In programming, stacks are vital for expression conversion (infix to postfix) and evaluation. They are extensively used in recursion, backtracking, and managing function calls within computer memory. Common software features like undo/redo mechanisms and browser history navigation also rely on stack logic. Ultimately, stacks provide an efficient way to manage data by focusing on the interface of operations rather than implementation details.
