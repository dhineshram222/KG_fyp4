I. Overview of Computer Networks

• Definition: A computer network consists of a group of computers connected through wires, optical fibers, or wireless links to share data, resources, and messages.
• Basic Purpose: To facilitate communication and allow multiple devices to interact within a single system.

II. Key Features of Computer Networks

• Communication Speed: Provides fast and efficient communication via email, messaging applications, and video/audio calls.
• File Sharing: Allows users to share various files using applications like Google Drive or WhatsApp.
• Centralized Backup: Data is stored on a main server, making it easy to back up folders and preventing data loss.
• Resource Sharing: Both software and hardware can be shared; for example, a software application can be installed once on a server and used by all connected machines.
• Security: Networks use authorization and authentication processes to ensure only permitted users access specific files.
• Scalability: New devices can be easily added to an existing restricted network area.
• Reliability: If a specific piece of hardware or software fails, alternative communication sources are usually available.

III. Classification of Networks (By Size/Area)

1. Personal Area Network (PAN)
• Range: Typically around 10 meters (30 feet).
• Usage: Used for personal devices like connecting a mobile phone, headphones, or printer to a laptop.
• Technology: Uses Bluetooth, Wi-Fi (Wireless PAN), or USB cables (Wired PAN).
• Pros/Cons: It is low cost and portable but has a very restricted coverage area.
2. Local Area Network (LAN)
• Range: Small, limited areas like a home, school lab, or office, covering up to 2 kilometers.
• Technology: Often uses Ethernet (cables) or Wi-Fi.
• Devices: Computers are typically connected via hubs or switches.
• Pros/Cons: Offers high speeds (up to 1,000 Mbps) but has high initial setup costs and limited privacy, as a LAN administrator can monitor user history.
3. Metropolitan Area Network (MAN)
• Range: Larger than a LAN, covering a city or town (5 km to 50 km).
• Usage: Interconnects multiple LANs, such as different branches of a bank or college within the same city,.
• Devices: Uses routers or bridges to connect the separate local networks.
• Pros/Cons: Provides high-speed connectivity and centralized management but has a complicated architecture and is expensive to maintain.
4. Wide Area Network (WAN)
• Range: Extends over large geographical areas like countries or continents (above 50 km),.
• The Internet: Recognized as the largest global WAN.
• Technology: Uses telephonic lines, fiber optic cables, or satellite links.
• Pros/Cons: Enables global business and quick data transmission over long distances, but faces significant security threats (viruses) and difficult troubleshooting.

IV. Modern Trends in Networking

• BYOD (Bring Your Own Device): A policy where employees are encouraged to bring their own personal devices to connect to the office network for work.
• Online Collaboration: Allows participants from different global locations (e.g., India, America, Australia) to meet and work together in real-time without being physically present in one spot.
• Cloud Computing: Offers on-demand availability of computer resources, especially data storage (e.g., Google Drive), allowing users to access files from anywhere in the world without carrying a personal computer.

V. Essential Intermediary Devices

• Hubs and Switches: Used primarily as intermediary devices to connect nodes within a Wired LAN.
• Routers and Bridges: Essential for connecting different local area networks together to form a MAN.
