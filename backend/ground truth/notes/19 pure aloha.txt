Aloha Protocol Overview

• Definition: Aloha is a type of Random Access protocol within the OSI model, originally designed for wireless local area networks (WLAN) but also applicable to shared wired mediums.
• Fundamental Principle: It allows multiple devices to share a single communication channel to transmit data frames.
• General Categories: There are two primary types of Aloha: Pure Aloha and Slotted Aloha.

1. Pure Aloha

• Transmission Mechanism: Stations are permitted to transmit data at any time they have a frame ready.
• Collision Characteristics:
    ◦ A collision occurs if two or more stations transmit frames simultaneously over the shared medium.
    ◦ Data is highly sensitive; if the first bit of a new frame overlaps with even the last bit of a frame that is almost finished, both frames are totally destroyed and rendered unusable.
    ◦ Collided frames are considered "garbled" and must be retransmitted.
• Recovery Strategy (Back-off Time):
    ◦ After transmitting, a station waits for an acknowledgment (ACK) from the receiver.
    ◦ If no ACK is received within a specific time, the station assumes a collision occurred.
    ◦ To prevent immediate repeated collisions, the station waits for a random amount of time, known as back-off time, before attempting to resend the data.
• Technical Metrics:
    ◦ Vulnerable Time: This is calculated as two times the frame transmission time (2×T fr ) because the channel must be clear during the current transmission and the preceding interval to avoid overlap.
    ◦ Maximum Efficiency: The maximum throughput is approximately 18% (specifically 0.184), which occurs when the number of stations wishing to transmit (G) is 0.5.

2. Slotted Aloha

• Innovation: Slotted Aloha was introduced by Robert in 1972 as an advanced version of Pure Aloha to improve communication efficiency.
• Transmission Mechanism:
    ◦ The shared medium's time is divided into discrete, synchronized time slots.
    ◦ Stations are restricted to transmitting data only at the beginning of each time slot.
• Impact on Performance:
    ◦ By forcing synchronization, the probability of frames overlapping is reduced, though collisions can still occur if two stations start at the beginning of the same slot.
    ◦ This protocol is globally synchronized, unlike Pure Aloha which is not.
• Maximum Efficiency: Due to the reduction in collisions, the maximum efficiency increases to 37%.

Key Comparisons

• Origin: Pure Aloha was developed at the University of Hawaii in 1972, while Slotted Aloha was introduced later that same year.
• Synchronization: Pure Aloha allows transmission at any time and lacks synchronization; Slotted Aloha requires all stations to be globally synchronized to specific time intervals.
• Reliability: The probability of successful transmission is significantly higher in Slotted Aloha compared to Pure Aloha.
• Collision Frequency: Pure Aloha experiences a higher number of collisions because there are no restrictions on when a station can start sending data.
